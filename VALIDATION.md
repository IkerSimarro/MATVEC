# MATVEC Validation

## Purpose
This document compares MATVEC's material recommendations against the
materials actually used on 6 well-documented historical aerospace systems.
The goal is credibility: a viewer who knows aerospace materials should be
able to read the table below and decide whether MATVEC's physics + matching
engine produce defensible recommendations.

## Methodology
For each historical vehicle:

1. **Envelope** is taken from primary published sources (flight manual,
   NASA technical report, or peer-reviewed monograph). All envelopes are
   single peak conditions, not full-mission averages — MATVEC analyses
   the worst-case load, so the envelope must capture peak Mach + peak
   thermal load.

2. **Historical material** is the primary structural / hot-face material
   actually flown. Where the vehicle used a *family* of related alloys
   (e.g., SR-71's various Ti grades), any member of that family counts
   as a match — MATVEC isn't expected to predict the specific lot a
   manufacturer chose, only that it lands in the right alloy family.

3. **Design lifetime** is taken from each vehicle's documented service
   life: SR-71 (3,000 h), X-15 (50 h), Concorde (25,000 h), Apollo CM
   (1 h, single mission), Space Shuttle Orbiter (25,000 h fleet
   accrual), CFM56-class HPT blade (25,000 h overhaul interval). The
   lifetime drives the Larson-Miller creep stage in the matching
   engine: at sustained-load lifetimes (≥1,000 h) materials must
   survive both the static thermal+structural snapshot AND the
   rupture-stress envelope at (T_wall, lifetime). This is what
   eliminates conventional Al 2024 / 7075 from sustained M=2 cruise:
   they survive 100 °C for one flight but creep their way to rupture
   at 25,000 h.

4. **Panel thickness** is set per vehicle for the 1D transient heat
   solver (Phase 7 rollout): SR-71 1.5 mm Ti skin, X-15 2 mm,
   Concorde 2 mm Al-Cu, Apollo CM 25 mm bonded honeycomb, Space
   Shuttle Orbiter 2 mm, CFM56-class blade 1 mm airfoil leaf. The
   transient solver runs whenever the per-mission flight duration
   is below 300 s (boost-coast trajectories, single-mission
   capsules); for sustained flights the static T_wall check is
   correct and the transient stage is a no-op. None of the six
   validation cases trigger the transient solver at the design
   point (all six are evaluated at the peak Mach / peak-heating
   moment, which is by definition the worst-case envelope the
   static check is built for). The transient stage is a separate
   guarantee for short-duration custom envelopes, not a validation
   case here.

5. **MATVEC match** runs the envelope through the full pipeline
   (`run_session`) and looks up the historical material in three places,
   in priority order:
   - **viable list** — load-bearing structural materials whose thermal,
     strength, AND creep margins all pass at the envelope's worst-case
     conditions. A hit here is a strong match (✓).
   - **TPS bucket with positive thermal margin** — ablators (AVCOAT,
     PICA), reusable TPS tiles (LI-900, AETB-8), and CMC heat shields
     (RCC). The matching engine routes these to a separate
     `tps_coatings` list because real designs pair them with a load-
     bearing substrate (the TPS itself is non-structural). A hit here
     with positive thermal margin is also a strong match (✓), because
     for a vehicle that genuinely uses TPS this is the architecturally
     correct outcome.
   - **marginal list** — materials that pass thermal but have <20%
     structural OR creep margin (or vice versa). A hit here is a
     weak match (⚠); real aerospace materials do operate in this band
     (Concorde's Al 2618 is the textbook case), but it signals MATVEC
     is recommending edge-of-envelope.

   No hit in any of the three is a miss (✗).

6. **Acceptance criterion:** at least 4 of 6 vehicles must produce a
   strong match (viable or TPS-with-positive-thermal-margin). The
   weaker target — 4 of 6 in the viable list specifically — would
   penalise vehicles like Apollo and the Shuttle Orbiter that are
   architecturally TPS-protected, where the historical material was
   never expected to be load-bearing.

## Per-vehicle interpretation

### SR-71 Blackbird

Top-of-list match. At Mach 3.2 / 25 km MATVEC computes a recovery-
temperature wall of ~607 K, well below the TPS-unlock threshold (1200 K),
so the matching engine works in the standard direct-evaluation mode and
the aircraft category's exclusion rules apply (no polymer composites
above Mach 2, density penalty for high-density alloys). The viable list
collapses to two titanium grades: `Ti-5Al-2.5Sn` and `Ti-6Al-2Sn-4Zr-2Mo`,
both Ti-6Al-4V cousins from the SR-71's actual alloy family. Conventional
nickel superalloys (Inconel 718, etc.) survive the thermal load but get
demoted by the aircraft category's specific-strength preference because
they are roughly twice as dense as titanium for similar strength. This
is exactly the trade Lockheed made: the SR-71 is ~93% titanium because
mass dominates at this scale, and titanium covers the wall-temperature
requirement with margin.

The historical-record divergence is finer than MATVEC resolves: the
specific Beta-21S and Ti-13V-11Cr-3Al grades used on early A-12
prototypes were chosen for thin-gauge formability rather than strength
or temperature ceiling. MATVEC doesn't model formability or weldability,
so it lands on the right family without picking the right grade.

### X-15

Match in viable list at rank #13. At Mach 6.7 / 30 km, MATVEC's
Sutton-Graves stagnation heating drives T_wall to roughly 1300 K — hot
enough that the score function (min of thermal and structural margin
fractions) over-rewards materials with very large thermal headroom,
pushing Carbon-Carbon and C/SiC CMC to the top despite the X-15 not
having actually used either. Inconel X-750, the historical primary
structure, lands at #13 with positive margins on both axes — it survives,
it's just outranked.

The real X-15 used Inconel X-750 not because it had the largest thermal
margin available but because it was the highest-strength alloy that
could be wrought, welded, and inspected with 1959-era manufacturing.
MATVEC's score function is conservative-but-margin-greedy and would
recommend ceramics that the X-15 program could not have built. The
ranking is wrong; the viability call is right.

### Concorde

Marginal hit on `2219-T87` (heat-resistant Al-Cu, Saturn V tank
alloy and the closest in-database analogue to Concorde's actual
Al 2618 / RR58). Under the new lifecycle-aware screening with
25,000 h design lifetime, **`2024-T3` is now correctly rejected
on creep** — its Larson-Miller rupture stress at 100 °C × 25,000 h
falls below the σ_required of ~117 MPa, which is exactly the
historical reason Hiduminium RR58 was developed in the 1960s.
2219-T87 survives marginally because its higher-temperature
creep capability sits closer to RR58's. This is a more honest
result than the pre-creep round, where 2024-T3 was wrongly flagged
as "marginal-but-survivable" at the same envelope.

Al 2618 (RR58), the heat-resistant aluminum specifically
developed for Concorde's M2.04 / 18 km cruise condition, is not in
materials_db.py. The closest chemical analogues — 2024-T3 (Al-Cu-Mg)
and 2219-T87 (Al-Cu) — both appear in the marginal list, indicating
they pass thermal but sit close to the structural margin threshold.
MATVEC's top-5 viable for this envelope is dominated by titanium grades,
which is technically correct (titanium also survives Concorde's wall
temperature) but ignores Concorde's mass-driven preference for aluminum
at the scale of a 78-tonne airliner.

This is the cleanest example of an honest model limitation: the materials
database does not include Al 2618 / RR58 as a distinct entry, and per
the project's standing rule we do not add to the database to chase
validation results. A reader interpreting this row should understand
that MATVEC is recommending a Ti-family aircraft for the Concorde
envelope, which is not what Concorde was built from but is technically
defensible at the M=2 cruise condition.

### Apollo Command Module

Strong match in the TPS bucket: AVCOAT lands at rank #1 of 10 in
`tps_coatings` with a +2090 K thermal margin — an enormous figure that
reflects the ablator's design margin, not its steady-state survival
temperature (ablators work by mass loss, not thermal equilibrium).
Carbon phenolic, PICA, and PICA-X all rank similarly high.

The viable list for Apollo's M22 / 70 km envelope is filled with
refractory metals (Niobium C-103, Tungsten) and CMCs (C/SiC, Carbon-
Carbon Composite) — these are the materials that would survive the
1410 K wall temperature steady-state, but no Apollo-era engineering
team would have built a manned capsule out of niobium. MATVEC's
TPS-bucket logic correctly captures the architectural reality: a
reentry capsule is built around an ablator, and the underlying
substructure (Apollo used aluminum honeycomb + stainless steel) is
shielded from the steady-state wall temperature by the ablator's
mass-loss cooling. The validation passes because the model recognises
this routing, not because the steady-state physics suddenly works for
ablators.

### Space Shuttle Orbiter

Strong match in the TPS bucket at rank #7 of 10: RCC (Reinforced
Carbon-Carbon, the actual material of the Shuttle's nose cap and wing
leading edges) shows a positive +47 K thermal margin at the M25 / 80 km
envelope. Higher-ranked entries above RCC are all ablators (AVCOAT,
Carbon phenolic, PICA, PICA-X, SLA-561V, SIRCA) which are not what the
Shuttle used — but RCC's lower rank reflects the fact that ablators
have more thermal headroom than reusable CMCs, not that RCC is the
wrong choice.

The viable list (which represents the substructure under the TPS) is
nickel superalloys, which is *not* what the Orbiter actually used —
the real substructure was aluminum 2024 / 2219, protected from the
wall temperature by the LI-900 and RCC TPS. MATVEC treats the case as
`vehicle_category="hypersonic_aircraft"` rather than `reentry`, which
is defensible (the Orbiter performed a hypersonic glide reentry, not a
capsule-style ballistic reentry) but it means the matching engine
doesn't run the substrate-mode pass that would surface aluminum at the
TPS-protected back-face temperature. Reading the row honestly: MATVEC
correctly identifies RCC as a viable TPS option, and a substrate-mode
re-run with a TPS-protected back-face soak would surface Al 2024 as
the substructural recommendation. The current top-of-list nickel
superalloys are an over-spec, not a wrong-family answer.

### CFM56-class HPT blade

Match in viable list at rank #39. The turbine override sets T_wall to
the hot-section gas temperature (1400 K) and the matching engine's
turbine-category exclusions correctly ban aluminum and polymer
composites; what survives are nickel superalloys, refractory metals,
and CMCs. CMSX-4, the historical single-crystal nickel for CFM56-class
HPT blades, lands at #39 of 43 viable materials.

The deep ranking is the score function again: at 1400 K the highest-
melting refractories (Tungsten, Niobium C-103) and CMCs (C/SiC, SiC/SiC)
have larger thermal margins than nickel single-crystals, so they
outscore CMSX-4 even though no real engine uses tungsten airfoils
(too dense, too brittle, oxidises catastrophically above ~600 °C).
The viability call is correct — CMSX-4 *is* survivable at this
envelope, which is exactly what the historical record says — but the
ranking is once again margin-greedy in a way that doesn't reflect the
multi-objective trade real turbine designers solve. The Pareto and
density-penalised ranking views in the UI handle this better than the
raw score does; the raw score is the conservative-feasibility filter,
not the design-recommendation engine.
